### Div Garg — score 7 — technical_explainer
https://www.linkedin.com/feed/update/urn:li:activity:7471299813666566144

**Summary:** Div explains that the AI-chip bottleneck is data movement, not compute, and the promising fix is processing-near-memory (PNM): compute where the data sits. Qualcomm, Samsung, Anker, and SK hynix are shipping PNM chips this year, but the real blocker is software: PNM compilers are hand-tuned per chip and per task, so performance collapses when either changes. His team works on generalizing that compiler layer.
**Key insight:** The gating factor for on-device/efficient inference is the compiler, not the silicon: without a general PNM compiler, each new chip or workload needs hand-tuning, which is why the hardware isn't in phones yet.
**Comment draft:**
> The compiler being the real bottleneck, not the silicon, is the part most coverage misses. Hand-tuned per-chip-per-task compilers don't scale, so a general PNM compiler layer is what actually unlocks this. Feels analogous to how CUDA mattered more than any single GPU.
**Content angle:** A post on "the compiler is the moat" for efficient inference: data movement vs compute, processing-near-memory, and why a general compiler layer (not the chip) is the thing that ships on-device AI.
